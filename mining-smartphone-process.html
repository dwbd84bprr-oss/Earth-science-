Which Minerals Make Each Part?

1. Screen & Display

Specialized minerals conduct electricity, generate vivid colors, and resist scratches.

  • Indium (from Sphalerite): used as Indium Tin Oxide (ITO) — a transparent, conductive layer that detects exactly where your finger touches.
  • Silicon (from Quartz): pure silicon dioxide creates the ultra-clear glass, chemically treated with Potassium and Aluminum ions ("Gorilla Glass") to resist shattering.
  • Rare Earths (Yttrium, Europium, Terbium): tiny amounts act as "phosphors" producing the brilliant red, green, and blue on OLED/LCD screens.

2. Electronics & Brains

The processor, memory, and wiring rely on highly conductive, stable metals.

  • Silicon (from Quartz): the foundation of the microprocessor — sliced into wafers where billions of transistors are etched.
  • Copper (from Chalcopyrite): the primary circuitry and wiring material, thanks to excellent conductivity.
  • Gold & Silver (native ore): microscopic coatings on connection points — flawless conduction, zero corrosion.
  • Tantalum (from Coltan): micro-capacitors that regulate power and prevent short-circuits.

3. The Battery

High energy density in a lightweight package.

  • Lithium (from Spodumene or brine pools): the core of Li-ion batteries — ions shuttle between electrodes to store and release energy.
  • Cobalt (from Heterogenite): keeps the cathode stable, safe, and cool during fast-charging.
  • Graphite (carbon mineral): the anode that holds lithium ions when fully charged.

4. Mic, Speakers & Vibration

Sound and haptics need incredibly strong magnets.

  • Neodymium, Dysprosium, Praseodymium (from Monazite/Bastnäsite): combined with iron and boron they form neodymium magnets — the strongest permanent magnets known — packed into tiny speakers, mic diaphragms, and the haptic motor.

Where Is Each Mineral Extracted?

Some minerals are mined directly as primary resources; others are only obtained as hidden byproducts of other large-scale mining operations.

The Main Minerals

Indium

Rarely mined on its own — almost exclusively a byproduct of zinc mining (sphalerite). China dominates with ~50–60% of primary supply; also South Korea, Canada, Japan, Peru, and Belgium (via imported zinc processing).

Lithium

Two methods: hard-rock mining (spodumene) and continental brine extraction. Australia is the largest hard-rock extractor; Chile and Argentina pump brine in the high-altitude "Lithium Triangle"; China and Zimbabwe are growing fast.

Cobalt

The most geographically concentrated: the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) produces over 70% of the world's cobalt, mined alongside copper and nickel. Russia, Australia, and Indonesia produce smaller shares.

The Secondary Minerals

Magnesium

Abundant in the crust — extracted from dolomite and magnesite, or directly from seawater and brines. China controls roughly 80–90% of world output; also Russia, Israel, and the United States.

Nickel

Mined from laterites and magmatic sulfides. Indonesia is by far the largest producer, scaling up massively for EV and smartphone batteries; also the Philippines, Russia, New Caledonia, Australia, and Canada.

Global Processing Note

Extraction (mining rock out of the ground) is not refining (purifying it into usable chemicals). While Australia and the DRC lead raw extraction, China commands the largest share of global refining for nearly all of these — especially lithium, cobalt, indium, and magnesium.

The Main Minerals

Indium — the invisible byproduct

No "indium mines" exist — it hides inside zinc sulfide ores. Conventional open-pit or underground mining extracts the zinc ore, which is roasted and smelted; indium concentrates in the residues, slag, and flue dust. Engineers then use acid leaching (sulfuric or hydrochloric acid) plus solvent extraction, and finally electrolysis turns it into metal bars.

Lithium — liquid brines vs. hard rock

Continental brines: mineral-rich saltwater is pumped into massive surface evaporation ponds; over 12–18 months the sun leaves a concentrated sludge, treated with sodium carbonate to create lithium carbonate. Spodumene: open-pit mining blasts out lithium-bearing rock, which is crushed, heated in kilns, and chemically leached.

Cobalt — sifting the leftovers

Extracted as a byproduct of giant copper or nickel mines. Raw rock goes through froth flotation (metal clings to bubbles and floats to the top), then is acid-leached or smelted at high temperature to isolate the cobalt.

The Secondary Minerals

Magnesium — rock blasting & sea water

From ore (magnesite/dolomite): open-pit mining, then Thermal Reduction — calcining at high heat and reacting with silicon in a vacuum to vaporize and condense pure magnesium. From brines/seawater: magnesium chloride is precipitated with lime, then electrolysis separates the metal from chlorine gas.

Nickel — laterites and sulfides

Laterites (surface): found under tropical topsoil (Indonesia); scraped from shallow open pits and cooked in high-temperature sulfuric acid tanks — HPAL (High-Pressure Acid Leaching). Sulfides (underground): deep shafts and tunnels; the ore is smelted in massive furnaces to melt away waste rock and create a concentrated nickel matte.
